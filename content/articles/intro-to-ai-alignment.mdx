## The Core Problem

An AI system is *aligned* when its behavior matches the intentions of the humans who designed and deployed it. This sounds straightforward. It is not.

The difficulty is not primarily technical. It is philosophical: we rarely have a precise, complete account of what we actually want. We rely on context, social norms, and implicit understanding to fill the gaps. A language model trained on human text can learn surface patterns without internalizing those underlying norms.

## Why Scale Changes Everything

Misalignment in a narrow tool — a thermostat, a spam filter — fails cheaply and locally. Misalignment in a system with broad capability and autonomous action can fail in ways that are hard to detect, hard to reverse, and potentially catastrophic.

This is the alignment problem in its strongest form: not "this model gave a wrong answer," but "this system pursued a coherent objective that diverged from human values in ways we didn't anticipate."

## Three Threads Worth Following

1. **Interpretability** — building tools to understand what is actually happening inside a model, rather than treating it as a black box.
2. **Scalable oversight** — designing evaluation processes that remain reliable as model capability exceeds human ability to directly verify outputs.
3. **Value learning** — approaches where the system infers human preferences from behavior rather than relying on an explicit reward specification.

Each thread represents a genuine research frontier with open problems.

## What Product Builders Should Know

If you are building products on top of large language models, alignment is not purely an academic concern. The same failure modes appear at smaller scale: models that are sycophantic, that confabulate with confidence, that optimize for a proxy metric in ways that undermine the goal the metric was meant to capture.

Building well means building with those failure modes in mind — from the prompt layer up.
